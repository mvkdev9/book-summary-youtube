# Made to Stick by Chip and Dan Heath

## 1. Long Detailed Summary for Blog

**Title:** Why Some Ideas Survive and Others Die: *Made to Stick*

**Introduction**
Why do urban legends spread effortlessly while important scientific facts are forgotten? Chip and Dan Heath identify the six traits of "sticky" ideas. If you want your message to be heard and remembered, you need to use the SUCCESs framework.

**The SUCCESs Framework**
1.  **Simple:** Find the core of the idea. It’s not about dumbing it down; it's about prioritizing. (e.g., Southwest Airlines: "The low-fare airline").
2.  **Unexpected:** Break a pattern. Grab attention by surprising the audience and then satisfying their curiosity.
3.  **Concrete:** Use sensory language. Avoid abstractions. Use "proverbs" and mental images (e.g., "A bird in the hand").
4.  **Credible:** Ideas need to carry their own credentials. Use "internal" credibility (vivid details) or "external" (experts). Try the "Sinatra Test" (if you can make it there, you can make it anywhere).
5.  **Emotional:** Make people care. Appeal to their self-interest or their sense of identity.
6.  **Stories:** Stories act as "mental flight simulators." They provide the "how-to" and the inspiration to act.

**Key Concept: The Curse of Knowledge**
Once we know something, we find it hard to imagine not knowing it. This makes it difficult to explain ideas to others. We assume they know the context we know.

**Conclusion**
Stickiness is not a natural gift. It is a set of tools you can apply to any message.

## 2. Long Form YouTube Video Script (10 Minutes)

**Title:** How to Make Your Ideas Memorable (The SUCCESs Framework)

**[0:00-2:00] The Kidney Heist**
*   **Host:** "You've heard the story. A guy goes to a bar, wakes up in a bathtub full of ice, and his kidney is gone. It's a total lie. But everyone knows it. Why? Because it’s a 'sticky' idea. Today we’re learning the science of why some ideas thrive and others die, based on *Made to Stick*."

**[2:00-5:00] Simple, Unexpected, Concrete**
*   **Host:** "The first three rules are the foundation. **Simple**: Find the core. If you say three things, you say nothing. **Unexpected**: Surprise your audience. If they can guess what you're going to say, they’ll stop listening. **Concrete**: Stop using corporate buzzwords. Talk like a human. Use examples people can see, touch, and feel."

**[5:00-7:30] Credible & Emotional**
*   **Host:** "To be **Credible**, you don't need a PhD. You need a story that passes the 'Sinatra Test'—one example that proves everything. To be **Emotional**, you have to make them feel something. Don't tell them 1 million people are starving; tell them about one little girl named Rokia. We care about individuals, not statistics."

**[7:30-9:00] Stories**
*   **Host:** "The final piece is **Stories**. A story is a mental flight simulator. It helps our brains practice what to do when we encounter a similar situation. If you want people to act, tell them a story."

**[9:00-10:00] Conclusion**
*   **Host:** "Use the SUCCESs framework. Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories. Now go make your ideas stick."

## 3. Short Form Video Scripts (30-60 Seconds)

**Script 1: The Curse of Knowledge**
*   **Audio:** "The biggest enemy of communication is the 'Curse of Knowledge.' Once you know something, you literally cannot remember what it was like to NOT know it. You start talking in jargon and abstractions that confuse everyone else. To beat the curse, you have to force yourself to use concrete examples and simple language. Don't assume they understand—show them."

**Script 2: Simple doesn't mean Stupid**
*   **Audio:** "Making an idea simple isn't about dumbing it down. It’s about 'finding the core.' If you have 10 priorities, you have zero priorities. A simple message is one that has been stripped of everything except the most essential truth. Think of Southwest Airlines: 'The low-fare airline.' That one sentence dictates every decision they make. Find your core."

**Script 3: Concrete vs. Abstract**
*   **Audio:** "Stop using buzzwords. 'Synergy,' 'Leverage,' 'Strategic Alignment'—your brain doesn't know what to do with those. Use 'Concrete' language instead. Talk about things you can see, smell, and touch. If you can't draw a picture of your idea, it's not concrete enough. Ideas that live in the clouds get forgotten. Ideas that live on the ground get done."

**Script 4: The Sinatra Test**
*   **Audio:** "Want to prove you're an expert? Use the Sinatra Test. In the song 'New York, New York,' he sings: 'If I can make it there, I'll make it anywhere.' If you can find one example of your work that was successful in the hardest possible environment, you don't need a resume. One 'big' win is more credible than a thousand small ones."

**Script 5: Use a Story**
*   **Audio:** "Why do we remember movies but forget lectures? Because of stories. A story is a 'Mental Flight Simulator.' It allows the listener’s brain to experience the situation without being there. If you want someone to learn a lesson, don't give them a list of rules. Tell them a story of someone who followed—or broke—those rules. Stories are the most powerful technology humans have ever invented."
